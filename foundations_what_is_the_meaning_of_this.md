## What Is the Meaning of This?

Numbers find us before we're even born. A pregnancy lasts approximately 40 weeks. A due date is calculated and circled on a calendar. In the delivery room, monitors track a mother's heart rate, blood pressure, oxygen levels — all numbers, measuring the most profound moments that mark the beginnings of our life.

A newborn may not receive a name right away (in some cultures, the naming ceremony is days after the child is born). But numbers arrive immediately: date and time of birth, weight in pounds or kilograms, length in inches or centimeters. These are recorded, documented, printed on a certificate. Before you knew your name, you had coordinates in time and space.

From that moment forward, milestones are marked numerically: the first day, the first week, the first month, and for the all-important birthday party, the first year. How tall are you now? How much do you weigh? Doctors plot growth on percentile charts which follow a logarithmic curve (rapid growth early, slowing with time). Parents record each milestone in baby books, annotated with dates and measurements. The calendar fills with checkups scheduled at 2 months, 4 months, 6 months, 1 year.

Your date of birth becomes an origin point — a personal epoch from which so much of life is measured. School enrollment depends on it. Legal rights are unlocked at specific ages: 16, 18, 21. Medical guidelines reference it. Retirement planning counts backward from it. That single date, a pair of numbers marking a day and a year, anchors decades of milestones yet to come.

For all the ways we disagree as a society — on politics, on religion, on how to raise children or organize society — we never argue about whether 2 comes after 1, or whether 3 plus 4 equals 7, or whether a triangle has three sides. Numbers are perhaps the closest thing we have to a universal language, a shared foundation that transcends culture, geography, and era. A proof that was valid in ancient Greece remains valid today. An series that converges in Alaska also converges in Zanzibar.
