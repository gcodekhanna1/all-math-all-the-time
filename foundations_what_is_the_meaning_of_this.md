## What Is the Meaning of This?

Numbers find us before we're even born. A pregnancy lasts approximately 40 weeks. A due date is calculated and circled on a calendar. In the delivery room, mother's heart rate, blood pressure, oxygen levels are all monitored.  These are all governed by numbers, measuring the most profound moments that mark the beginnings of our life.

A newborn may not receive a name right away (in some cultures, the naming ceremony is days after the child is born). But numbers arrive immediately: date and time of birth, weight in pounds or kilograms, length in inches or centimeters. These are recorded, documented, printed on a certificate. Before you knew your name, you had coordinates in time and space.

From that moment forward, milestones are marked numerically: the first day, the first week, the first month, and for the all-important birthday party, the first year. Ever curious Ffriends and relatives ask: how tall are you now? How much do you weigh? Doctors plot growth on percentile charts which follow a logarithmic curve (rapid growth early, slowing with time). Parents record each milestone in baby books, annotated with dates and measurements. The calendar fills with checkups scheduled at 2 months, 4 months, 6 months, 1 year.

Your date of birth becomes an origin point.  As with all points of origin, so much of your life is measured relative to that point. School enrollment depends on it. Legal rights are unlocked at specific ages: 16, 18, 21. Medical guidelines reference it. Retirement planning counts backward from it. That single date, a pair of numbers marking a day and a year, anchors decades of milestones yet to come.

For all the ways we disagree as a society, whether its politics, religion, how to educate children or organize society, we never argue about whether 3 plus 4 equals 7, whether a triangle has three sides, or whether the limit of $1/x$ is zero as $x \rightarrow\ \infty$. A proof that was valid in ancient Greece remains valid today. An series that converges in Alaska also converges in Zanzibar.

Numbers are perhaps the closest thing we have to a universal language, a shared foundation that transcends culture, geography, and era.
